📚 PART 1 · THE WILLOW TREE THAT TOOK FIVE YEARS TO ANSWER ONE QUESTION

In the early 1600s, Jan Baptist van Helmont ran one of the most patient experiments in the history of science. He planted a five-pound willow tree sapling in exactly 200 pounds of dried soil, covered the soil to keep out dust, and watered it faithfully for five years. At the end, he weighed everything again: the tree had gained about 164 pounds, and the soil had lost only about two ounces. His conclusion was almost right and almost completely wrong at the same time — he reasoned that since the soil barely changed, the tree's added mass must have come entirely from the water he'd added. He had correctly ruled out soil as the main source of a plant's mass, a genuinely important result nobody had proven before, but he had no way to know about the gas in the air that plants actually build most of their mass from. That gas is carbon dioxide, and untangling its role took another century and a half of further experiments by other scientists.

Photosynthesis (from Greek: photo, "light," + synthesis, "putting together") is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria capture light energy and use it to build sugar out of carbon dioxide and water, releasing oxygen as a byproduct. The word itself tells you the whole story once you know the roots: light energy is literally what puts the sugar molecule together. This happens mainly in a plant's leaves, in structures called chloroplasts, using the green pigment chlorophyll to absorb light. The sugar produced is the plant's own food — the matter it uses to build new roots, stems, and leaves — which means Van Helmont wasn't entirely wrong that water mattered; he simply couldn't see the other half of the equation happening invisibly, drawn from the air.

📚 PART 2 · WHY "PLANTS NEED SUNLIGHT" IS ONLY ONE THIRD OF THE ANSWER

It is tempting to think of photosynthesis as mainly a light problem: give a plant enough sun and it will thrive. Real plants do not work that way. Photosynthesis has three genuine requirements at once, light, carbon dioxide, and water, and all three have to be present for the reaction to run at all. The rate is not set by however much of the three inputs a plant has on average. It is set by whichever single input is scarcest at that moment, called the limiting factor. A greenhouse with brilliant light, plenty of water, but almost no carbon dioxide in the air will barely photosynthesize, no matter how bright the lamps are.

💡 WHAT THIS WIDGET IS SHOWING
Notice that raising the two inputs you did not touch never rescues the rate once one input hits zero. The process is not averaging three numbers together. It is being held back entirely by whichever single input is lowest, the limiting factor, the same idea you will see again later this unit when other systems depend on more than one resource at once.

This gives you a fuller picture than "plants need sunlight." A plant with unlimited sunlight still cannot photosynthesize in a sealed jar with no carbon dioxide, and it still cannot photosynthesize in dry soil no matter how bright the day is. All three inputs have to show up together.

📚 PART 3 · TESTING THE LIGHT REQUIREMENT WITH A REAL LEAF

A classic plant investigation covers part of a leaf, waits for the plant to use up the starch already stored there, then tests both the covered and uncovered sections with iodine, which turns blue-black in the presence of starch, to show that light is required to make new starch. That iodine test needs a chemical most homes don't have and takes longer than a week to run properly, so this week's notebook entry has you analyze real results from this exact investigation instead of trying to run it at home.

Van Helmont spent five years being careful and still missed half the answer, not because he was careless, but because carbon dioxide is invisible and nobody in his century had a reason to suspect a gas could become a tree.
