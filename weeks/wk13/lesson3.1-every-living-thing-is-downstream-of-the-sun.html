Lesson · Matter, Energy, and Feeding Relationships in Living Systems
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Every calorie you have ever eaten was sunlight first.
Trace the energy in almost any meal back far enough and you land in the same place: sunlight, captured by a plant. A cow eats grass that grew using sunlight; you eat the cow, or the grass's grain, or an animal that ate the grass-eating animal. Every step in that chain loses some usable energy as heat, which is exactly what the Second Law of Thermodynamics (a term you don't need to memorize yet, but the idea is worth knowing) predicts: energy transformations are never perfectly efficient. That is why a food web nearly always has far more plants than plant-eaters, and far more plant-eaters than the animals that eat them — each level up the chain, there is simply less usable energy left to support life.

📚 PART 1 · WHO EATS WHOM, AND WHY IT MATTERS

Producers (plants, algae, some bacteria) capture energy directly from sunlight and build it into food. Consumers get their energy by eating producers or other consumers — herbivores eat producers, carnivores eat other consumers, omnivores eat both. Decomposers (fungi and many bacteria) chemically break down dead organisms and waste at the molecular level, releasing the matter locked inside back into the soil, air, and water so producers can use it again. Detritivores (earthworms, many insects) work alongside decomposers by physically consuming and fragmenting dead material into smaller pieces first — they don't chemically break it down the way decomposers do, but shredding it into smaller, more exposed pieces makes the decomposers' chemical work faster. Without decomposers (and the detritivores that help them along), matter would get permanently locked up in every organism that ever died, and the whole system would grind to a halt within a few generations. A food web is really several of these chains overlapping, because most real organisms eat, and are eaten by, more than one thing.

↑ Widest at the bottom, narrowest at the top: usable energy decreases at each level. Most of the energy available at one level is lost as heat before it reaches the next, which is why a food web needs far more producers than top predators.
Decomposers aren't a tier of their own — they act on every level of this pyramid, recycling matter from dead organisms and waste at each tier back to the producers at the base.

💡 CONNECTION · BACK TO WEEK 11
The Law of Conservation of Energy from Week 11 did not stop applying once you left Physical Science. A food web is one enormous, ongoing demonstration of that same law: energy is never created inside an ecosystem, only captured (by producers), transferred (through feeding), and eventually released as heat. Living systems don't get an exception to physics — they are one of physics's most elegant applications.

🏃 VR ACTIVITY 3.1 · BUILD THE WEB (SC.7.L.17.1) · SOLO VR · ~25 MIN

What you're doing: Physically constructing a food web by connecting organism objects with energy-flow arrows, since a food web is fundamentally a spatial, connected structure that's easier to build than to draw.

How to join: Join this week's VR session using code CS2-WEB-13, in the Ecosystem Gallery — Grassland location. You'll start at the center clearing.

What to do, in order:

  1. Collect the ten organism objects scattered around the clearing (grass, grasshopper, mouse, snake, hawk, rabbit, fox, mushroom fungi, soil bacteria, sunflower).
  2. Arrange them in the air using the arrangement tool, placing producers lowest and predators highest.
  3. Use the drawing tool to draw energy-flow arrows from each organism to whatever eats it, building a connected food web rather than a single chain. Include at least one arrow from a dead-organism marker to a decomposer.
  4. Place a note card on one producer, one consumer, and one decomposer, correctly labeling its role.
  5. Record a 90-second recording walking through your completed web, tracing one full path from producer to top predator and explaining the decomposer's role.

What to submit: Your recording and a snapshot of your completed web.

Time: About 25 minutes.

⚠️ FACILITATOR NOTE
Watch for webs that are really just a single chain with no branching — a real web needs at least one organism with two arrows in or out to count as connected rather than linear.
🎯 SUCCESS CRITERIA
All ten organisms placed and connected; producer/consumer/decomposer roles correctly labeled; recording correctly traces one full energy path.
