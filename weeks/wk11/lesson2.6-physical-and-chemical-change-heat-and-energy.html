📚 PART 1 · A WEIGHT THAT TOLD THE TRUTH

Antoine Lavoisier worked in Paris in the 1770s, and he did something almost no chemist before him had done systematically: he weighed everything, before and after every reaction, using some of the most precise balances ever built. Chemists before him believed that when something burned, it lost a mysterious fire substance called phlogiston. Lavoisier's scale told a different story. When metals burned inside a sealed container, the total mass of everything inside, container included, never changed, even though the metal itself gained mass as it turned to ash. The burning metal was not losing something. It was combining with a gas from the air: oxygen.

Lavoisier's careful weighing did more than explain one reaction. It overturned phlogiston theory completely and established one of the most important ideas in all of science: matter is neither created nor destroyed in an ordinary physical or chemical change. It only rearranges.

The candle got shorter and the air inside changed completely, but the jar as a whole never got lighter or heavier. Every atom that left the candle is still somewhere inside the jar.

A physical change alters an object's form without changing what it is actually made of: ice melting, paper tearing, sugar dissolving. You can usually reverse it. A chemical change produces a genuinely new substance with different properties: wood burning to ash and smoke, iron rusting, an egg cooking. It is typically much harder, or impossible, to reverse. The reliable evidence of a chemical change includes a color change, gas production (bubbling), a temperature change that is not just from mixing hot and cold, a new odor, or a permanent change in properties. No single sign proves it by itself (whipping cream changes texture but stays cream), which is why scientists look for a pattern of evidence together, not just one clue.

📚 PART 2 · CHECKING THE LAW YOURSELF

Lavoisier's law makes a specific, checkable claim: in a closed system, the mass you start with equals the mass you end with, no matter what reaction happens in between. That is not a vague rule. It is strict enough to test on a virtual balance, using a reaction you already know: baking soda and vinegar.

💡 WHAT THIS WIDGET IS SHOWING
An open container that seems to lose mass has not broken the Law of Conservation of Mass. Carbon dioxide gas escaped into the air the moment the reaction fizzed, and once you count that gas, the two totals match exactly. The same lesson applies to this week's at-home investigation: a scale can only weigh what stays inside the container, so the setup you choose changes what the scale can tell you, even when the underlying law never changes.
📚 PART 3 · WHERE THE MATTER, AND THE ENERGY, GOES NEXT

Physical and chemical changes are not the only place this idea, that nothing simply disappears, shows up. The same logic applies to energy. When you touch something hot, heat does not appear from nowhere, and it does not vanish when things cool down. Heat always flows from a warmer object to a cooler one, never the other way, until both reach the same temperature. When a solid changes into a liquid, the energy you keep adding does not always raise the temperature. Sometimes it goes into changing how the particles are arranged instead, which is why temperature holds steady for a while during melting even though heat is still flowing in.

The same is true when a machine runs, from a falling domino to a spring launcher. The energy involved does not run out. It transforms: stored energy becomes motion, motion becomes more motion at a collision, and eventually motion becomes thermal energy and sound at friction and impact, always accounted for, never destroyed. This week's two VR sessions let you test both ideas directly, with your own hands and your own data, before you ever see them written as a rule in a textbook.

💪 BEFORE YOU MOVE ON
