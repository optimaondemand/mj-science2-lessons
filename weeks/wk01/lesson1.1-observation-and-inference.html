A locked room, a puddle on the floor, and a knocked-over houseplant — what actually happened?
Welcome to your first week of Comprehensive Science 2. Before you can reason well about rocks, cells, or energy this year, you need one habit of mind first: telling the difference between what you actually observed and what you're inferring from it. That habit is the toolkit every unit this semester depends on.

📚 PART 1 · OBSERVATION VS. INFERENCE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Right now, before we teach any of this: how sure are you that you could explain the difference between an observation and an inference to a friend? (No wrong answer — just check in with yourself.)

The Locked Room

Picture a locked room: a puddle of water on the floor, an open window, and a houseplant that has clearly been knocked over. What happened? You don't know — you weren't there. But you can look at the room and build a story that fits everything you see. That story is an inference: a reasonable explanation built from evidence, not a guess pulled from nowhere. The puddle, the open window, and the toppled plant are observations — things you directly detected with your senses or a tool.

Confusing the two is one of the most common mistakes in science, and in life: people report an inference ("the cat did it") as if it were an observation ("I saw the cat do it"). Sort the room below into what you actually observed and what you're only explaining:

👁️ I CAN DIRECTLY DETECT THIS (Observation)

The floor is wet
The window is open
The plant is on its side, soil scattered nearby

💭 I AM EXPLAINING THIS (Inference)

Rain blew in through the window
The cat knocked the plant over
Someone left in a hurry and forgot to close the window
💡 TRY IT IN YOUR NOTEBOOK
Before moving on, jot down one more possible inference that would also fit these three observations. There's more than one story that fits the same evidence — that's exactly why scientists have to keep testing.
📚 PART 2 · THEORY VS. LAW

Two words people mix up constantly

Science runs on the observation/inference distinction at a much larger scale. A scientific law describes a pattern that always happens under certain conditions. The Law of Superposition (you'll meet it again in Week 9) is a law: it describes what happens.

A scientific theory is a well-supported explanation for why it happens, built from a large body of evidence, tested repeatedly, and revised when new evidence demands it. In everyday speech "theory" means a hunch. In science, a theory is closer to the strongest, most tested kind of explanation there is — germ theory, atomic theory, and the theory of plate tectonics are not shaky guesses. They are ideas that have survived a great deal of attempted disproof.

⚡ TRY IT · LAW OR THEORY?
For each statement, decide whether it's describing a scientific law or a scientific theory.
1. "In an undisturbed rock sequence, older layers sit below younger ones."
2. "All matter is made of atoms, and this explains why substances combine in fixed proportions."
3. "Objects fall toward Earth at the same rate regardless of their mass (ignoring air resistance)."
📚 PART 3 · A CASE FROM HISTORY

The map that beat the microscope

For most of human history, people explained disease with "bad air" (miasma). It was a reasonable inference from the available evidence — disease often followed foul-smelling swamps and sickrooms. It took John Snow, in 1854 London, mapping cholera deaths street by street, to show the pattern actually pointed to a single contaminated water pump, not the air at all.

Snow didn't have a microscope powerful enough to see the cholera bacterium. He had something almost as powerful: a map, careful observation, and the discipline to let the evidence overturn a comfortable old idea. That discipline is what this whole unit is about.

⚠️ COMMON MISTAKE
Aristotle believed the senses, carefully used, could disclose the truth of nature. He was also confident, on the strength of pure reasoning alone, that heavier objects fall faster than light ones — a claim nobody tested for nearly two thousand years, until Galileo tested it directly. Reasoning without testing is exactly the trap this course is training you to avoid.

So what? Every unit this semester will ask you to build explanations from evidence — about rocks, cells, energy, and matter. The habit you're building this week (name what you actually observed, then name what you're inferring from it, and don't blur the two) is the single most useful habit in this entire course.
