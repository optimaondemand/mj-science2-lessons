📚 PART 1 · A PROPERTY THAT BELONGS TO THE SUBSTANCE

Density is mass divided by volume, and it is a number that belongs to the substance itself, not to the size of the particular chunk you happen to be holding. A gold ring and a gold bar have wildly different masses and volumes, but identical density, because density is a property of gold, not of "this specific piece of gold." That's what makes density such a powerful identification tool: you can compare a tiny sample to a reference table and make a real claim about what something is made of.

It's worth being precise about a common mix-up: mass is the amount of matter in an object, and it doesn't change no matter where you are. Weight is the force gravity exerts on that mass, and it does change — you'd have the same mass on the Moon as on Earth, but you'd weigh about a sixth as much, because the Moon's gravity pulls less hard. Density, conductivity, solubility, magnetism, melting point, and boiling point are all properties that describe the substance itself rather than the specific sample, which is why scientists lean on them so heavily for identification.

Reading the ladder
  • Each object settles at the layer matching its own density — not its size.
  • A bead sinks through oil and alcohol but rests on top of honey.
  • A grape hovers near the dish-soap/water boundary.
  • A bottle cap floats on almost everything except honey.
