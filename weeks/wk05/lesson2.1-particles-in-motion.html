📚 PART 1 · THE UNCUTTABLE PARTICLE

Democritus, a Greek philosopher writing around 400 BC, proposed that if you cut any substance in half, and in half again, and kept going, you would eventually reach a piece that could not be cut any further — an atomos, Greek for "uncuttable." He had no microscope, no lab, no evidence beyond pure reasoning.

He turned out to be right about something important — that matter is built from discrete, countable particles rather than being infinitely divisible — even though his specific claim that those particles were themselves uncuttable turned out to be wrong. It took over two thousand years and far better instruments to confirm that matter really is particle-based, and to discover that atoms themselves are made of still-smaller parts after all.

📚 PART 2 · THREE STATES, ONE STORY

Every solid, liquid, and gas you've ever touched is made of tiny particles in constant motion. The differences between the three states come down to two things: how tightly packed the particles are, and how much freedom they have to move.

SOLID

Packed close, vibrating in place. Holds its shape.

LIQUID

Still close, but sliding past each other. Takes the shape of its container.

GAS

Far apart, moving independently in every direction. Expands to fill its space.

Temperature is related to the average kinetic energy of those particles — roughly, how much energy of motion they carry on average. Add heat, and particles gain kinetic energy, move faster, and push farther apart; a solid's particles vibrate hard enough to break free of their fixed positions (melting), or a liquid's particles gain enough energy to escape into a gas (boiling).

⚡ TRY IT · STATE OF MATTER
For each description, identify the state of matter.
1. Particles are close together but slide past one another; the substance takes the shape of its container but keeps the same volume.
2. Particles are far apart and move independently in every direction, expanding to fill any container.
3. Particles are packed closely and mostly vibrate in place, so the substance holds a fixed shape.
