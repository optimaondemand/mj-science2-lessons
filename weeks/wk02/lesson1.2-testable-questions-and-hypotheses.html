📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
"Why do I like the color blue?" is a real question. Science can't answer it. Here's why — and what kind of question it can answer.
Not every question is a testable one. This lesson teaches you to spot the difference, and to turn a fuzzy curiosity into a sharp, testable question with a hypothesis attached.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to turn a curious observation into a testable question and a hypothesis.
2
I will be able to explain why empirical evidence is the foundation every scientific explanation stands on.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your science notebook
📚 PART 1 · TESTABLE OR NOT?
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of something you've wondered about this week — anything. Do you think science could actually design a test to answer it? (No wrong answer.)

"Why do I like the color blue?" is a real question, but no experiment settles it. "Does the color of light affect how fast a plant grows?" is testable: you can change one thing (light color), hold everything else constant, and measure a result. Seventh grade science lives in that second kind of question.

💡 PRO TIP
A quick test: can you name one thing you'd change, and one thing you'd measure? If yes, it's probably testable. If your answer is "it depends how someone feels" or "it depends what's beautiful," it probably isn't.
📚 PART 2 · THE QUESTION-TO-HYPOTHESIS LADDER

Every investigation you run this year climbs the same three rungs, bottom to top:

RUNG 1
Curious Observation: "I noticed the bean plant by the window looks bigger than the one on my desk."
RUNG 2
Testable Question: "Does the amount of sunlight a bean plant gets affect how tall it grows in two weeks?"
RUNG 3
Hypothesis: "If a bean seedling gets less sunlight, then it will grow more slowly, because sunlight is required for the plant to produce the energy it needs to grow."

Notice the hypothesis's shape: a change, an expected effect, and a reason (if / then / because). A hypothesis is not a wild guess, and it is not required to be correct — a "wrong" hypothesis that gets tested honestly still produces real scientific knowledge.

Now climb one yourself

2. Which is a well-formed hypothesis for that question?
📚 PART 3 · EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE, THREE WAYS

Everything a hypothesis eventually answers to is empirical evidence: the accumulated body of direct observations of the natural world. This is what separates science from other ways of knowing — a scientist has to go check.

Biology, geology, and physics all share this commitment to empirical evidence, but they check it differently: a biologist might dissect and observe, a geologist might read rock layers laid down over millions of years, and a physicist might build a controlled lab setup. Same commitment to evidence, three different toolkits, because the questions themselves demand different tools.

⚠️ COMMON MISTAKE
John Snow's 1854 cholera map is this week's case study in your VR activity. The popular explanation of the time ("bad air") sounded sciencey but was never actually observed — it was assumed. Snow's plain, boring-looking map was the real empirical evidence. Don't confuse "sounds impressive" with "was actually checked."

So what? Every investigation you run this year — the density lab in Week 6, the plate-tectonics VR lab in Week 9, the food-web VR lab in Week 13 — starts by climbing this same ladder. Get comfortable with it now and the rest of the semester gets easier.
