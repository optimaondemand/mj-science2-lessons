📚 PART 1 · A GAMBLE THAT PAID OFF

In 1869, Dmitri Mendeleev arranged the sixty-three known elements on cards by atomic mass and noticed something remarkable: their properties repeated in a pattern, over and over, like notes climbing back to the same pitch in a higher octave. Where the pattern broke, Mendeleev made a bold move: rather than force a bad fit, he left gaps in his table and predicted that undiscovered elements would eventually fill them, going so far as to describe the physical properties those missing elements should have.

When gallium was discovered in 1875 and germanium in 1886, both matched Mendeleev's predictions astonishingly well. That is one of the strongest kinds of evidence a scientific idea can produce: not just explaining what's already known, but correctly predicting something nobody has found yet.

The modern periodic table organizes elements by atomic number (protons) rather than mass, but the underlying pattern Mendeleev spotted is the same one you'll use all year: elements in the same column (group) share similar chemical behavior, because they have the same number of electrons available for bonding — at least for main-group elements; this pattern is less clean for the transition metals in the middle of the table. Elements in the same row (period) show properties that shift gradually as you move across.
