Respiration: Photosynthesis in Reverse, and Why Every Cell Needs It
Lesson · Two Processes, One Matched Pair
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Last week a plant turned light into sugar. This week, find out what every living cell, plant or animal, does with that sugar to actually put it to use.
Photosynthesis and cellular respiration are a matched pair: one builds sugar, the other breaks it back down to release usable energy. Today you will compare the two processes side by side, clear up a common mix-up between "respiration" and "breathing," and see why matter keeps cycling between the two processes while energy does not.

2

3
I will be able to explain why matter cycles between photosynthesis and respiration while energy, once released as heat, does not cycle the same way.

📚 PART 1 · THE MATCHED PAIR
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before you read further: how sure are you that breathing and cellular respiration are the same thing? (No wrong answer, just check in with yourself.)

Look back at the photosynthesis equation from last week and notice something striking: cellular respiration's overall inputs and outputs are the complement of photosynthesis's — not a simple reversal of the same steps run backward, but a genuinely different set of chemical pathways that happens to start where photosynthesis ends and end where photosynthesis starts. Where photosynthesis uses light energy to build sugar from carbon dioxide and water, cellular respiration breaks sugar back down using oxygen, releasing usable energy (not sunlight this time, but energy the cell can actually run on) along with carbon dioxide and water as byproducts. Plants do both processes; animals, which cannot photosynthesize, rely entirely on respiration, using sugar they get by eating. This is the deepest reason a food web works the way it does: every organism, plant or animal, needs respiration to unlock the energy stored in sugar, whether that sugar was self-made or eaten.

💡 VISUAL · PHOTOSYNTHESIS AND RESPIRATION, A COMPLEMENTARY PAIR
PHOTOSYNTHESIS
carbon dioxide + water + light energy → sugar + oxygen
CELLULAR RESPIRATION
sugar + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + usable energy
The outputs of each process are the inputs of the other. These are complementary overall equations, not reverse steps of the same pathway.

It's worth being precise about a common misconception: "respiration" is not the same as "breathing." Breathing moves oxygen into your body and carbon dioxide out of it; cellular respiration is what actually happens inside each individual cell, using that oxygen to release energy from sugar. You could say breathing is the delivery system, and cellular respiration is what the delivery is for.

⚡ TAP EACH CARD TO REVEAL
Photosynthesis: Inputs
Carbon dioxide, water, and light energy. Light energy is the input that makes photosynthesis different from respiration.
Photosynthesis: Outputs
Sugar and oxygen. These outputs become the inputs cellular respiration needs to run.
Cellular Respiration: Inputs
Sugar and oxygen, the very outputs photosynthesis just produced.
Cellular Respiration: Outputs
Carbon dioxide, water, and usable energy the cell can actually run on. Not sunlight this time.
Breathing vs. Cellular Respiration
Breathing moves oxygen in and carbon dioxide out of your body. Cellular respiration is what happens inside each cell using that oxygen to release energy.
Which organisms perform each process?
Plants perform both photosynthesis and respiration. Animals only perform respiration, using sugar they get by eating.
📚 PART 2 · MATTER CYCLES, ENERGY DOESN'T

Cross-unit connection: This complementary pair is the same conservation-of-matter-and-energy story from Weeks 11 and 13, now running at the scale of a single cell — but matter and energy don't behave identically here. Matter genuinely cycles between the two processes: the same carbon atoms move from air to sugar to air again. Energy does not cycle the same way — it enters the system as light, gets transformed and transferred through photosynthesis and respiration, and is eventually released as heat that leaves the system for good. Next week's carbon cycle is really a story about matter cycling; energy, once it becomes heat, does not come back around to be used again.

🎯 ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
MATTER
Cycles. The same carbon atoms travel: air → sugar → air, again and again.
ENERGY
Flows one way. Light in, transformed through both processes, then released as heat and gone for good.
🧬 VR ACTIVITY 3.3 · THE INSTRUCTIONS INSIDE EVERY CELL
Solo VR · About 25 minutes

What you're doing: The course map's Life Science unit is built entirely around bioenergetics, but CPALMS requires seventh graders to understand heredity: that every organism carries hereditary instructions (DNA) organized into genes on chromosomes, passed from parent to offspring. This activity, continuing across Week 16, covers that ground.

How to join: Join this week's VR session using code CS2-DNA-15, in the Cellular Structures Hall — Nucleus Chamber Location. You'll start at the nuclear membrane.

What to do, in order:
  1. Enter the room-scale nucleus and locate the coiled chromosome floating in the center.
  2. Use the zoom-in interaction on one chromosome to reveal its DNA double-helix structure, and walk along its length to see individual gene segments highlighted and labeled (seed shape, flower color, pod color, and two others — the same simple, single-gene pea-plant traits Mendel studied).
  3. At the Inheritance Table, examine the placed parent-and-offspring organism pair (a pea plant, matching the same species Mendel used) and match which parent trait appears in the offspring.
  4. On the whiteboard, sketch a simple diagram showing the correct nesting: a chromosome is a tightly coiled package containing many genes; each gene is a specific instruction-carrying segment of DNA; DNA is the molecule genes are made of, and it carries the instructions passed from parent to offspring.
  5. Record a 90-second recording explaining, in your own words, what DNA actually is, how it relates to genes and chromosomes, and how it connects a parent to its offspring.

What to submit: Your recording and a photo of your whiteboard diagram.

Time: About 25 minutes.

💡 FACILITATOR NOTE
Scholars often use "chromosome," "gene," and "DNA" interchangeably, and often get the nesting backward. The correct relationship: DNA is the molecule; a gene is a specific instruction-carrying segment of DNA; a chromosome is a tightly coiled package of many genes. Also watch for scholars treating eye color or height as single-gene traits — those are complex, multi-gene, environment-influenced traits, which is exactly why this activity uses simple single-gene pea-plant traits instead.
✅ SUCCESS CRITERIA
Diagram correctly nests genes within chromosomes and shows genes as segments of DNA (not DNA nested within genes); recording clearly explains inheritance in the scholar's own words using a single-gene trait example.
