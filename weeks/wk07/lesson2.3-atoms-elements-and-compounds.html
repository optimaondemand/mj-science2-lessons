📚 PART 1 · A BEAM, A FOIL, AND A SURPRISE

In 1909, Ernest Rutherford's team fired a beam of tiny, positively charged particles at an extremely thin sheet of gold foil, fully expecting them to sail straight through. The leading atomic model of the day pictured the atom as a soft, evenly spread blob of positive charge with electrons scattered through it, like fruit in a pudding. Almost all the particles did pass through undisturbed. But a small number bounced straight back — proof that something small, dense, and positively charged had to be concentrated at the atom's center.

That "something" was the nucleus, and the gold-foil experiment overturned the pudding model in favor of the atom you learn today: a tiny, dense nucleus of protons and neutrons, surrounded by a much larger cloud of electrons.

1904: Plum Pudding
Positive charge spread evenly, electrons embedded throughout.
1911: Nuclear Atom
Dense positive nucleus, electrons at a distance.
One unexpected result (particles bouncing back) was enough to overturn a model everyone believed.

Protons carry positive charge, electrons carry negative charge, and neutrons carry none. The number of protons is what makes an atom the element it is: change the proton count and you have a genuinely different element, not just a different version of the same one.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL · KEY TERMS
Proton
Positively charged particle in the nucleus. Its count determines the element.
Neutron
Uncharged particle in the nucleus. Changes affect the isotope, not the element.
Electron
Negatively charged particle in a cloud around the nucleus. Its balance with protons sets the atom's charge.
Element
A pure substance made of only one kind of atom (oxygen, gold, carbon).
Compound
Atoms of different elements bonded in a fixed ratio — a genuinely new substance (water is always two hydrogen, one oxygen, never any other ratio).
