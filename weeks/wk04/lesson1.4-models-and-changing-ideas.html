📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A subway map is not an accurate picture of a city. It's wrong on purpose. Why doesn't that make it a bad map?
Scientific models work the same way: they leave things out and simplify others, and that's what makes them usable at all. This week you'll learn to ask two questions of any model, and meet a scientist who was right for decades before anyone believed him.

📚 PART 1 · EVERY MODEL LEAVES SOMETHING OUT

A subway map straightens curves, exaggerates distances, and doesn't care where north is. That's not a flaw — a map with every real detail would be useless for the one job it has: helping you get from one station to another. Scientific models work the same way. The skill this week is asking two questions of any model: what does this model help me understand, and what does it leave out or get wrong?

Consider the atomic model you'll meet in Week 7: tiny colored balls representing protons, neutrons, and electrons orbiting like a mini solar system. That picture helps you understand charge, mass, and basic structure. It also completely misrepresents how electrons actually behave (they don't orbit in neat circles at all). Both things are true at once — knowing both is what makes you a sophisticated user of the model, rather than someone quietly misled by it.

⚡ TRY IT IN YOUR NOTEBOOK · PREDICT, THEN CHECK
Before reading further: name one benefit and one limitation of a weather-forecast map (the kind with sun and cloud icons over a country map).
▶  Write your answer in your notebook, then click to compare
One reasonable answer: Benefit — it quickly shows you the general pattern (a storm system moving east) without needing to understand raw atmospheric data. Limitation — it can't show you the exact minute rain starts on your street, because it's simplified down to icons over a whole region.
📚 PART 2 · THE SCIENTIST WHO WAS RIGHT TOO EARLY

Scientific knowledge is not a fixed list of facts handed down from on high. It's a living conversation, and it changes when new evidence forces a change. For decades, geologists dismissed Alfred Wegener's 1912 idea that continents had once been joined and drifted apart. The matching coastlines of South America and Africa looked like a coincidence to most of his peers, and Wegener couldn't explain what force could possibly move something as massive as a continent.

He was mocked, and he died in 1930 on a Greenland expedition still convinced he was right and still unable to prove the mechanism. It took until the 1960s, and evidence from the ocean floor that Wegener never lived to see, before plate tectonics became one of the most solidly established theories in all of science.

1912

Wegener's map of matching coastlines. A pattern, but no mechanism — and no one believed him.

1960s ONWARD

Seafloor spreading at a mid-ocean ridge: the mechanism, found decades later, on evidence Wegener never saw. You'll build on this exact story in Week 9.

💡 GOING DEEPER
Being wrong about the mechanism did not make Wegener a bad scientist. Refusing to look at new evidence would have. Knowledge changing is not a weakness of science — it's science working exactly as it should.

So what? When you meet a strange, half-formed, or downright wrong-sounding idea in science this year, your job isn't to dismiss it or accept it uncritically. It's to ask what evidence supports it, what evidence would change it, and whether the debate is actually still open.
