A Punnett square is a complete, checkable probability tool, not a guessing exercise. Each parent contributes one allele per box: line up Parent 1's alleles across the top and Parent 2's alleles down the side, and each of the four inside boxes shows one equally likely offspring genotype. Notice above that changing just one parent's genotype visibly changes every offspring probability at once.

In the VR session, you'll work out this exact method by hand for a different trait: a cross between a heterozygous purple-flower plant (genotype Pp) and a white-flower plant (genotype pp). Use the same logic the generator above uses: line up each parent's two alleles, fill in all four boxes, then count. Make sure you use the specific genotypes, Pp and pp, and not just “purple x white” — a cross is only a determined probability problem once the genotypes are specified.

Reading a Pedigree Chart

A pedigree chart tracks a trait across generations using squares (males) and circles (females). A filled shape shows someone affected by the trait. For a recessive trait, a person can carry one copy of the recessive allele, pass it to a child, and never show the trait themselves, because their other allele is dominant. That person is a carrier. You can identify a likely carrier on a pedigree by finding an unaffected parent who nonetheless has an affected child: the only way that child got two recessive alleles is if the unaffected parent supplied one of them.

Mitosis vs. Meiosis

Mitosis takes one cell and produces two genetically identical cells; it's how your body grows and repairs damaged tissue. Meiosis takes one cell and produces four genetically varied sex cells (sperm or eggs); it's how organisms produce offspring that are similar to, but not identical to, either parent. The number of resulting cells (two vs. four) and whether those cells are identical or varied are the two differences to watch for in the VR session's looping animations.

Biotechnology: Humans Directing Heredity

Biotechnology is what happens when humans intentionally manipulate heredity instead of leaving it to chance. Selective breeding chooses which organisms reproduce based on a desired trait, generation after generation. Cloning (as with Dolly the sheep) produces a genetically identical copy of an existing organism. Genetic engineering directly edits an organism's genes, such as bacteria engineered to produce human insulin. All three are ways of steering heredity on purpose, at very different scales.

📚 PART 2 · WHAT THE ROCKS REMEMBER (SUPPORTS VR ACTIVITY 3.4b)
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Picture opening a rock layer and finding a fossil unlike anything alive today. How would you feel about it? (No wrong answer — just check in with yourself.)

The fossil record uses the same rock-layer reasoning you learned in Week 9: in an undisturbed sequence, older layers sit below younger ones. When you look at fossils across many layers of the same rock sequence, you're looking across time, oldest at the bottom, youngest at the top.

The horse-family fossil record is a classic example, and it does not look like a straight ladder from small ancient horse to modern horse. It looks like a branching family tree: several coexisting, now-extinct horse-relative lineages (some multi-toed, some single-toed, of varying sizes) alongside the one line that led to the modern single-hoofed horse.

Youngest layer — modern single-hoofed horse lineage continuestop
Middle layers — several coexisting lineages: some multi-toed, some single-toed, varying sizesmiddle
Older layers — earliest small, multi-toed horse-relativesbottom, oldest

Several of those side-branch lineages went extinct instead of continuing. This branching pattern, and thousands like it across many species, is exactly what the scientific theory of evolution predicts: gradual change over deep time across multiple coexisting lineages, not a single species steadily transforming into the next one toward a predetermined goal. One fossil sequence by itself is evidence consistent with that prediction; it is one piece among many, not proof by itself.
