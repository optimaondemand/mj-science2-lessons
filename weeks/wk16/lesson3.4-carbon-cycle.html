One Atom's Long Trip: The Carbon Cycle
Lesson · Matter That Never Leaves, Only Relocates
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A carbon atom in your body right now could, a thousand years from now, be part of a tree, a fish, the air over another continent, or a layer of limestone.
This is the carbon cycle: the same finite supply of carbon atoms moving continuously between the atmosphere, living things, the ocean, and rock, never created or destroyed, only relocated and rebonded. Today you'll trace that journey and connect it back to a rule you already know: matter is conserved.

📚 PART 1 · DOORS, NOT WALLS
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before you read further: where do you think most of the carbon atoms making up your body right now were, one thousand years ago? (No wrong answer — just check in with your first guess.)

A carbon atom in your body right now could, a thousand years from now, be part of a tree, a fish, the air over another continent, or a layer of limestone. This is the carbon cycle: the same finite supply of carbon atoms moving continuously between the atmosphere, living things, the ocean, and rock, never created or destroyed, only relocated and rebonded. Photosynthesis pulls carbon dioxide out of the air and locks its carbon into sugar; respiration (in plants and animals alike) releases it back into the air; decomposers release the carbon locked in dead organisms; and over geologic time, some carbon gets buried and pressed into fossil fuels or rock, where it can sit for millions of years before a volcano, a fire, or a factory chimney sends it back into the air.

This cycle is the clearest possible demonstration that the boundary between “living” and “nonliving” chemistry is not a wall — it's a set of doors carbon atoms pass through constantly. The same conservation-of-matter principle that made Lavoisier's sealed jar balance in Week 11 is what makes a carbon-cycle diagram balance: every arrow into a box is matched, over time, by arrows back out.

📚 PART 2 · ONE CYCLE, FOUR RESERVOIRS

Every carbon atom on Earth lives in one of four reservoirs at any given moment: the atmosphere (as carbon dioxide), living things (as sugars, fats, and proteins), the ocean (dissolved or built into shells), or rock and fossil fuels (locked away, sometimes for millions of years). The map below shows the named process that moves carbon along each arrow.

Follow the loop clockwise from Atmosphere: photosynthesis moves carbon into Living Things, respiration moves it right back. Decomposition and burial move carbon from Living Things down into Rock/Fossil Fuels. Ocean absorption pulls carbon from the Atmosphere into the Ocean, and sedimentation moves it from the Ocean into Rock. Combustion is the one arrow that can send carbon straight from Rock/Fossil Fuels back up to the Atmosphere, sometimes after millions of years underground.
⚡ PLACE IT ON THE TIMESCALE · HOW LONG DOES THIS STEP TAKE?
For each carbon-cycle event, place it on the timescale from fastest to slowest.
1. A deer breathes out carbon dioxide shortly after eating a leaf.
2. Decomposers slowly break down a fallen log, releasing its stored carbon back into the air.
3. Layers of shells and ocean sediment slowly compact into limestone rock.
4. A power plant burns coal that formed from ancient buried plants, releasing that stored carbon in a quick combustion reaction.
