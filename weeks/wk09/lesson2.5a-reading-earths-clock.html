📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Rock is not a fixed, finished thing. It's matter cycling on a timescale too slow to watch directly, but not too slow to reconstruct.
This is a shorter lesson, but the ideas in it (the rock cycle, plate tectonics, and the two clocks geologists use to date rock) carry real weight this year. Read it once, then head into this week's two VR activities where you'll use these ideas directly.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to describe the rock cycle and plate boundaries, and explain how superposition and radioactive dating are used together to establish geologic time.
📚 PART 1 · THE ROCK CYCLE, DRIVEN FROM BELOW

Molten rock cools into igneous rock; weathering breaks any rock down into sediment, which compacts into sedimentary rock; heat and pressure transform existing rock into metamorphic rock; and metamorphic or sedimentary rock can melt again to restart the cycle.

🌋 Molten rock cools
🪨 Igneous rock
↓ Weathers to sediment
🪨 Sedimentary rock
🔥 Heat + pressure
🪨 Metamorphic rock → melts again

What drives this over millions of years is plate tectonics, the same mechanism Wegener's continents were missing back in Week 4. Where plates pull apart (divergent), new ocean floor forms. Where they collide (convergent), mountains rise or one plate dives beneath another. Where they grind past each other (transform), earthquakes result. Heat flowing up from Earth's interior drives this plate motion in the first place.

Predict first, in your notebook: if two plates are colliding (a convergent boundary), what surface event is most likely — an earthquake, a mountain range, or new ocean floor? Write your answer, then click to check.
A convergent boundary is most associated with mountain building (or one plate diving beneath another). Transform boundaries are most associated with earthquakes; divergent boundaries with new ocean floor. All three boundary types can produce some earthquake activity, but the surface event each is best known for is the one listed here.
📚 PART 2 · TWO CLOCKS, USED TOGETHER

Once rock layers are in place, geologists read their age two different ways. The Law of Superposition says that in an undisturbed sequence of sedimentary layers, older layers sit below younger ones — a relative clock, useful for ordering events but not for putting a number of years on them.

Radioactive dating supplies the number: certain unstable "parent" isotopes decay into "daughter" isotopes at a fixed, measurable rate (the half-life). Many kinds of decay actually change the parent atom's proton count, turning it into a genuinely different element as it decays — what stays reliable isn't the proton count, but the decay rate itself. By measuring the ratio of parent to daughter isotopes trapped in a mineral since it formed, and knowing that isotope's half-life, scientists calculate an absolute age. Superposition orders events; radioactive dating puts years on them, which is why geologists use both, not either one alone.

Predict first: a rock sample starts with 100% parent atoms. After enough time passes that only 25% parent atoms remain (and 75% have decayed to daughter atoms), how many half-lives have passed? Write your guess, then click to check.
Two half-lives. After one half-life, 50% parent atoms remain. After a second half-life, half of that 50% decays, leaving 25% parent atoms. Each half-life always cuts the remaining parent amount in half again, not by a fixed percentage of the original.

None of this happens in a vacuum: human activity now reshapes Earth's surface on its own timescale. Deforestation, urbanization, erosion, and changes to water and air quality are all measurable, present-day impacts on the same planet these geologic processes built.
