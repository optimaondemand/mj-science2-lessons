📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
You just spent six weeks proving that matter and energy follow strict rules. Today you meet something that looks like it might break every one of them: something alive.
It doesn't. A tree, a tadpole, and you are all, underneath everything else, still made of atoms following the same rules you spent Unit 2 establishing. 📚 PART 1 · LIFE OBEYS THE SAME RULES

Over the last six weeks you built a toolkit for the physical world: density and physical properties, atoms and the periodic table, mixtures and solutions, and two laws that anchor everything else — conservation of mass and conservation of energy. A ball rolling to a stop doesn't lose its energy; it transfers it. A sealed jar with a burning candle inside doesn't lose mass; it rearranges it. Those laws didn't stop being true the moment you turn your attention to something alive.

Every living thing you will study for the rest of this semester is, at bottom, a very sophisticated way of managing energy and matter without breaking the rules you just spent Unit 2 establishing. A plant does not create energy from nothing when it turns sunlight into sugar; it captures energy that already exists, streaming down from a star ninety-three million miles away, and repackages it into a form its own cells — and every animal that eats the plant — can use. Nothing about being alive exempts an organism from conservation of mass and energy. It only means an organism has evolved remarkably good machinery for directing where that mass and energy go.

Notice the shape of the arc: bioenergetics first (how living things capture and use matter and energy), then heredity (how that living machinery gets copied and passed on), then evolution and ecology (how that machinery changes over time and interacts with other living machinery around it).
