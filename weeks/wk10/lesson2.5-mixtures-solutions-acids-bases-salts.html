📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Stir sand into water and you can filter it back out unchanged. Mix an acid and a base and you get something you'd put on your food. Both are mixtures, or reactions, of matter, but they don't work the same way at all.

📚 PART 1 · MIXTURES, SOLUTIONS, AND WHAT STAYS THE SAME
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before you read on: stir salt into water until it disappears, and stir sand into water until it turns cloudy. Are these two mixtures the same kind of thing? (No wrong answer — just check in with yourself.)

Stir sand into water and you can filter it back out unchanged — nothing about the sand or the water was altered, they were just combined. That is the defining feature of a mixture: the substances keep their own identities and can, at least in principle, be separated back out. A solution is a special, thoroughly mixed kind of mixture where one substance (the solute) disperses evenly into another (the solvent) at the particle level. Salt fully dissolved in water is a solution; sand suspended in water is not, because the sand particles are far larger and will eventually settle.

This is different again from a compound like water itself, where hydrogen and oxygen have chemically combined into something with entirely new properties that no amount of filtering will ever separate back into the original elements. A mixture (sand and water) can be pulled apart by physical means; a compound (water) cannot — only a chemical reaction breaks it back into hydrogen and oxygen.

💡 HELPFUL TO KNOW
Muddy water and sugar water look similarly cloudy or clear at a glance, but only one is a true solution. Muddy water is a suspension of large particles that will settle if you let it sit; sugar water is a solution all the way down to the particle level and will never settle out on its own. This is the exact distinction this week's VR sorting activity will ask you to make.
📚 PART 2 · ACIDS, BASES, SALTS, AND THE pH SCALE

Acids, bases, and salts are a special case of this classification that matters for everyday life as much as for chemistry class. An acid (lemon juice, vinegar, stomach acid) is often described as tasting sour, and a base (baking soda solution, soap, ammonia) as feeling slippery — but those descriptions are for recognizing acids and bases you already know are safe household foods, never instructions for identifying an unknown substance. Scientists and students alike use indicators and pH meters for exactly that reason, never taste, smell, or touch.

The pH scale runs from 0 (strongly acidic) through 7 (neutral, like pure water) to 14 (strongly basic), and it's a logarithmic scale — each whole step is a tenfold change in acidity, not a small one. When an acid and a base react completely, they neutralize each other and produce a salt plus, often, water: table salt itself is the product of hydrochloric acid reacting with sodium hydroxide, two substances you would not want to touch directly, combining into something you put on your food.

💪 ONE IDEA TO CARRY FORWARD

A mixture keeps its ingredients' own identities, no matter how thoroughly they're stirred together. A solution is the most thoroughly mixed case of that; a compound is a different thing entirely, changed at the chemical level. Acids and bases sit on the same pH scale, and neutralizing them doesn't erase them, it builds something new: a salt.
