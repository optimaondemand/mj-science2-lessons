📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A drought on a tiny Galápagos island once shifted an entire finch population's beak size within a single generation, while scientists watched it happen.
Today you'll learn the three ingredients natural selection needs to work, why it explains extinction as a mismatch between traits and a changing environment rather than a species "failing to try," and why the same finches that puzzled Darwin in 1835 are still teaching biologists new things nearly two centuries later.

📚 PART 1 · A MECHANISM CAUGHT IN THE ACT

When Charles Darwin visited the Galápagos Islands in 1835, he collected finches from several different islands without initially realizing how significant their differences were — it was only later, when an ornithologist examined his specimens, that the pattern became clear: each island's finches had a beak shape closely matched to that island's available food. Decades later, biologists Peter and Rosemary Grant spent over forty years directly measuring these same finches on the island of Daphne Major, and during a severe drought they watched, in real time, the average beak size of an entire finch population shift within a single generation, because birds with slightly larger, stronger beaks were better able to crack the tough seeds that survived the drought. This is natural selection observed happening, not just inferred after the fact.

Natural selection requires three ingredients: genetic variation already existing within a population (not every finch has the exact same beak), an environmental pressure that makes some variations more useful than others (a drought that leaves only tough seeds), and a resulting difference in survival and reproduction (birds with the useful trait leave more offspring). Over many generations, this shifts the population's traits — it does not change any individual bird during its own lifetime. When an environment changes faster than a species can produce or select for useful variation, the population can shrink and eventually disappear entirely: extinction. The fossil record from last week and the mechanism you're learning this week fit together like a lock and its key — the fossil record shows that species changed over time, and natural selection is the best-supported explanation scientists have for how.

🌐 SOLO VR ACTIVITY 3.5a · ~25 MIN

Survive the Drought

SC.7.L.15.2, SC.7.L.15.3

What you're doing: Role-playing one feeding round of Galápagos finches to feel, rather than just read about, why variation and environmental pressure together produce differential survival — the raw material natural selection is built from. This single round shows survival advantage in one generation; the notebook assignment right after this activity extends the reasoning across multiple generations, which is where natural selection actually happens.

How to join: Join this week's VR session using code CS2-SELECT-17A, in the Galápagos Island Simulation location. You'll start at the finch aviary.

What to do, in order:

  1. Choose one of five finch avatars, each with a different beak-size label, and use it to "collect" seeds scattered around the terrain within a two-minute timer (small, easy seeds and large, tough seeds are both present).
  2. Check your score against the placed results board showing how each beak size performed during the drought scenario (large-beak finches scored highest, because most remaining seeds were tough).
  3. Walk to the Extinction Case Wall and read the placed case card on the dodo, noting the environmental change (introduced predators, habitat loss) it could not adapt to quickly enough.
  4. Record a 90-second recording explaining your one-round result and the dodo's extinction in terms of a mismatch between traits and a changing environment.

What to submit: Your recording. Time: About 25 minutes.

Facilitator note: Some scholars will describe their finch as "trying" to grow a bigger beak. Redirect firmly: the beak size was fixed before the drought; only survival and reproduction changed. Also be clear that this one round shows differential survival, not natural selection itself — natural selection is what happens when that survival advantage plays out across generations, which is what Assignment 3.5a asks scholars to reason through.
Success criteria: Recording correctly explains why large-beak finches survived better this round and connects extinction to inability to adapt in time.
Right after this VR session: Assignment 3.5a, "Across Generations, Not Just One Round," asks you to sketch how the population's beak sizes would shift over three generations if the large-beak survivors were the only finches that reproduced, and to explain why that shift happens at the population level, not inside any one bird.
📚 PART 2 · LIFE TOGETHER IN AN ECOSYSTEM

Extinction is not the only way organisms shape each other's survival. Long before a population disappears, it is already tangled up in relationships with the other organisms around it, some helpful, some harmful, and some that barely register at all. This week's second VR activity sends you back into the Ecosystem Gallery to sort five of the most common relationship types found in almost any ecosystem, and to practice something scientists have to do constantly: pointing to the specific piece of evidence that actually explains a pattern, rather than settling for the pattern by itself. Try sorting these five examples yourself before you go.

⚡ TRY IT · MUTUALISM, PARASITISM, PREDATION, COMPETITION, OR COMMENSALISM?
Read each example and classify the relationship between the two organisms.
1. A bee collects nectar from a flower while pollinating it in the process.
2. A tick attaches to a deer and feeds on its blood, causing the deer harm.
3. A lion chases down and kills a zebra for food.
4. Two wolves in the same pack both try to catch the same rabbit, and only one succeeds.
5. A small bird rides on the back of a grazing animal, eating insects stirred up by its movement, without helping or harming the grazer.
🌐 SOLO VR ACTIVITY 3.5b · ~25 MIN

Living Together: Symbiosis and Limiting Factors

SC.7.L.17.2, SC.7.L.17.3

What you're doing: Returning to the Ecosystem Gallery from Week 13 to examine the relationships between organisms beyond "who eats whom," and the factors that limit how large a population can grow.

How to join: Join this week's VR session using code CS2-SYMBIOSIS-17B, in the Ecosystem Gallery — Grassland location. You'll start at the relationship exhibit.

What to do, in order:

  1. Walk through five paired-organism displays (a bee and a flower; a tick and a deer; a lion and a zebra; two wolves competing for the same rabbit; a bird and a grazing animal it rides on). At each, read the note card description and sort the pair into the correct labeled bin: mutualism, parasitism, predation, competition, or commensalism.
  2. Move to the Limiting Factors Table and examine a population graph for a rabbit population that grows quickly, then flattens out, alongside four additional placed data panels showing that same time period's food-supply levels, disease-case counts, rainfall, and available shelter for this population. Identify which of the four accompanying data panels actually changes in a way that lines up with where the population graph flattens — the population graph by itself only shows that the population hit a carrying-capacity limit, not which specific factor caused it.
  3. On the whiteboard, write one sentence for each of the five relationship types, using an example from the exhibit.
  4. Record a 90-second recording explaining your five sorting decisions and which data panel's pattern actually supports your limiting-factor conclusion.

What to submit: Your recording and whiteboard photo. Time: About 25 minutes.

Facilitator note: Commensalism is consistently the hardest of the five to distinguish from mutualism — the key question to prompt with is "does the second organism actually benefit, or is it just unaffected?" On the limiting-factor task, make sure scholars point to the specific data panel that actually changed at the right time, not just their favorite guess among the five general categories — the population graph alone doesn't determine which factor was responsible.
Success criteria: At least 4 of 5 pairs correctly classified; limiting-factor conclusion is tied to a specific supporting data panel, not just to the shape of the population graph alone.
Before the Fall Semester Cumulative Exam: Assignment 3.5b, "Synthesis Notebook: Selection, Extinction & Ecosystem Relationships," asks you to apply this week's four ideas, trait-frequency data, extinction reasoning, comparing two relationship types, and identifying a limiting factor with evidence, independently in writing.

💪 BEFORE YOU MOVE ON

No finch ever decided to grow a bigger beak, and no dodo failed to try hard enough. Populations shift because some individuals already carried a useful trait and survived to pass it on; species vanish when the environment changes faster than that shifting can keep up. Carry that same habit of mind into this week's ecosystem relationships: name the mechanism, and point to the specific evidence, rather than settling for a story that only sounds right.
